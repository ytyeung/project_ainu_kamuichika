## Chironnup yaieyukar, “Towa towa to” 
## 狐が自ら歌った謡「トワトワト」
## The Fox's Self Chant “Towa towa to”

Towa towa to
One day, I went to the seaside to gather food.
Stepping through the crushed stones, Towa towa to.
Stepping through the woody chips, Towa towa to.
As I walked, I looked ahead and saw
A whale had washed ashore on the beach.

Men and women were in fine dresses,
Rejoicing and dancing over the gift of the sea [[1]](#f_1_2). 
Cutting meat, carrying meat, back and forth.
Respectful chief offering thanks and prayers for the gift of the sea.
The beach was in black colour, filling with people sharpening knives.
Seeing this, I was delighted.
“I must hurry to get there, 
I want to receive even a little bit.”
I thought.
Then I shouted, “Ononno! Ononno! [[2]](#f_2_2)” 

Stepping through the crushed stones, Towa towa to.
Stepping through the woody chips, Towa towa to.
I went closer and closer, but when I arrived,
Contrary to all expectations,
What I thought was a whale washed ashore,
Was just waste of dogs on the beach.
A large dump of waste with nasty smell.
I had mistaken for a whale.

I thought that people were rejoicing and dancing for the gift of the sea,
Cutting and carrying the meat.
But indeed they were crows,
Pecking and scattering the waste dump,
Flying around, flying left and right.

I was angry.
“You dim-eyed, worthless fool, Towa towa to.
You dull-eyed, wicked fellow, Towa towa to.
You smelly thing beneath the tail, Towa towa to.
You rotten thing beneath the tail, Towa towa to.
You resin coming out of your butt, Towa towa to.
You dirty water coming out of your butt, Towa towa to.
What a way to see things!”

Then again,
Stepping through the crushed stones, Towa towa to.
Stepping through the woody chips, Towa towa to.
Walking along the seaside,
Looking forwards,
And saw a boat.
In the boat, 
Two people were exchanging condolences [[3]](#f_3_2).

“Oh! What a sudden tragedy
Has caused their sadness?
Perhaps someone has capsized with the boat.
I must hurry,
Coming closer and hearing their sayings.”
Thinking this, I shouted loudly, 
“Hokokse! [[4]](#f_4_2)”

Stepping through the crushed stones, Towa towa to.
Stepping through the woody chips, Towa towa to.

I ran quickly, and I found the fact.
I thought that it was a boat.
Indeed it was a rock on the beach.
I thought that they were people.
Indeed they were two large cormorants,
Stretching and shrinking their long necks,
As if they were exchanging condolences.

“You dim-eyed, worthless fool, Towa towa to.
You dull-eyed, wicked fellow, Towa towa to.
You smelly thing beneath the tail, Towa towa to.
You rotten thing beneath the tail, Towa towa to.
You resin coming out of your butt, Towa towa to.
You dirty water coming out of your butt, Towa towa to.
What a way to see things!”

Then again,
Stepping through the crushed stones, Towa towa to.
Stepping through the woody chips, Towa towa to.
I went up the river with quick steps.
Far away at the upstream,
I saw two women,
Standing in the shallow, weeping together.
Seeing this, I was surprised.
“Oh, what is the tragedy,
What is the bad news [[5]](#f_5_2) that they weep sadly? [[6]](#f_6_2)
I must hurry and arrive quickly, 
To hear their description.”

Thinking that,
Stepping through the crushed stones, Towa towa to.
Stepping through the woody chips, Towa towa to.
I went up the river with quick steps, and saw 
In the middle of the river, there were two fish traps.
Being hit by the current,
The piles of the two fish traps were shaking.
I thought that there were two women bowing, 
Looking up each other, and weeping.

“You dim-eyed, worthless fool, Towa towa to.
You dull-eyed, wicked fellow, Towa towa to.
You smelly thing beneath the tail, Towa towa to.
You rotten thing beneath the tail, Towa towa to.
You resin coming out of your butt, Towa towa to.
You dirty water coming out of your butt, Towa towa to.
What a way to see things!”

Then again,
I went upstream of the river.
Stepping through the crushed stones, Towa towa to.
Stepping through the woody chips, Towa towa to.
I went home with quick steps.
I looked at the direction.
For some reason,
My house was burning!
The smoke, rising into the sky,
Similar to piled up clouds.

When I saw that, 
I was so surprised that I almost lost consciousness.
I screamed in a woman's voice [[7]](#f_7_2).
I jumped up.
From the other side, someone screamed loudly,
“Hoo-i! [[8]](#f_8_2)” and ran to my side.
My wife, with a surprised expression on her face,
“Husband, what is wrong?” she said in breathless.

I observed more carefully.
I thought that my house was on fire.
My house was here as it always be.
No fire, no smoke.

My wife was milling grain.
At that moment when the wind blew strongly, 
The winnowed millet bran was being blown away.
I saw it as smoke.
I went out to look for food but found none.
On top of that,
I screamed so loudly that my wife was surprised,
Throwing away the winnowed millet with the basket.
We have nothing to eat tonight.

I was furious,
Threw myself onto the floor,
And went to sleep.

“You dim-eyed, worthless fool, Towa towa to.
You dull-eyed, wicked fellow, Towa towa to.
You smelly thing beneath the tail, Towa towa to.
You rotten thing beneath the tail, Towa towa to.
You resin coming out of your butt, Towa towa to.
You dirty water coming out of your butt, Towa towa to.
What a way to see things!”

This was the tale told by the Fox Chief about himself.

## Footnote         
         
### Chiri     
   
<span id="f_1_2"> [1] isoeonkami. iso refers to marine products, eonkami refers to thanking for something. It is believed that when a whale is stranded on the shore, it is the sea deity bringing it and pushing it ashore as a gift to humans. Therefore, at such times, respected individuals would dress formally and offer prayers facing the open sea.</span>

<span id="f_2_2"> [2] ononno. These are words spoken in unison by people welcoming those returning from hunting in the mountains or at sea with their catch.</span>

<span id="f_3_2"> [3] uniwente... A ritual performed after major disasters such as floods, fires, volcanic eruptions, or in cases where someone has been eaten by a bear, fallen into the sea or river, or been injured or died due to any unusual event. During this ritual, people exchange words of condolence while pointing the tips of spears or swords at each other. When a village has suffered a disaster, many representatives from neighboring villages gather to perform the ritual, even if it is just one person from each village.</span>

<span id="f_4_2"> [4] hokokse... A male cry for help to the gods during uniwente or when a major unusual event has occurred. It is limited to men.</span>

<span id="f_5_2"> [5] ashur refers to unusual news, ek means coming. This refers to when someone traveling far from the village becomes ill or dies, and a messenger is sent from that place to inform their hometown of the incident, or when someone comes to the village from another village to report the death of someone or the occurrence of some unusual event. The messenger is called ashurkorkur (the person carrying the unusual news). When ashurkorkur approaches the village, they first shout loudly hokokse. Upon hearing this, the villagers also shout loudly and go to the edge of the village to greet them and inquire about the unusual news.</span>

<span id="f_6_2"> [6] uchishkar... Crying together. This is a greeting among women, such as when they meet after a long separation, when a close relative has died, or when someone has narrowly escaped a great danger. Women hold hands or embrace each other's heads or shoulders and cry.</span>

<span id="f_7_2"> [7] matrimimse (a woman's cry)... In case of an emergency or uniwente, men shout hokokse in a thick voice, while women cry hoo-i in a thin voice. It is said that women's voices are higher and louder than men's voices, so they reach the ears of the gods first. Therefore, in the event of a sudden incident, even men will make a thin voice like women and shout two or three times.</span>

<span id="f_8_2"> [8] peutanke... Has the same meaning as rimimse, but this is a commonly used word, while rimimse is a slightly more difficult word.</span>
