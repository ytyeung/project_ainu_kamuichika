## Chironnup yaieyukar, “Haikunterke Haikoshitemturi”       
## 狐が自ら歌った謡「ハイクンテレケ ハイコシテムトリ」      
## The Fox's Self Chant “Haikunterke Haikoshitemturi”       
      
Hai Kunteleke Hai Koshitemutori      
      
Upon the cape of the land, the cape of the gods,      
Where was my seat.      
      
One day, I went out and gazed.      
The sea was calm.      
Upon the sea,      
Okikirmui, Shupunramka, and Samayunkur [[note 1]](#f_additional_2_3)        
Were on the same boat.       
Three of them, were hunting on the sea.      
Seeing this,      
The wicked thoughts within me stirred.      
      
Upon this cape, the cape of the land, the cape of the gods,      
I ran up and down, back and forth, with light steps,       
Twisting my waist, swinging my limbs.      
In a heavy tone, as if breaking wood into pieces,       
I cried, “Pau, pau! [[1]](#f_1_3)”      
Staring at the source of river,       
Summoning the demon of storm.      
Then, from the source of river,       
Fierce wind, a whirlwind, burst,       
And rushed into the sea.      
Immediately, the sea turned upside down.      
      
The boat of Okikirmui and his companions,       
Where the inshore and offshore intercepted,      
Encountered the sudden upheaval,      
Spinning around in the waves.      
The great wave, as huge as a mountain,      
Crashed over the boat.      
Then, Okikirmui, Samayunkur, and Shupunramka,      
Struggled to raise their voices, and row the boat.      
The small boat was like a falling leaf,      
Blown by the fierce wind,      
Nearly overturned.      
Admirable, the men were strong,       
Gliding the small boat through the wind, upon the waves.      
      
Seeing this,       
My wicked thoughts stirred again.      
I ran up and down, back and forth, with light steps,       
Twisting my waist, swinging my limbs.      
In a heavy tone, as if breaking wood into pieces,       
I cried, “Pau, pau!”      
Cheering on the Devil of Storm, devoting all my energy.      
      
After a while,       
Samayunkur finally collapsed in exhaustion with bleeding hands.      
Seeing this,       
I laughed secretly.      
      
Then, again,       
Devoting all my energy,      
Running up and down, back and forth, with light steps,       
Twisting my waist, swinging my limbs.      
In a heavy tone, as if breaking wood into pieces,       
Cheering on the Devil of Storm.      
      
Okikirmui and Shupunramka,      
Encouraged each other and rowed the boat bravely,      
But after a while,       
Shupunramka collapsed in exhaustion with bleeding hands.      
Seeing this,      
I laughed secretly.      
      
Then, again,       
I ran up and down, back and forth, with light steps,       
Twisting my waist, swinging my limbs.      
Yelling in a heavy tone, as if breaking wood into pieces,      
Devoting all my energy.      
      
But Okikirmui did not show signs of fatigue.      
Wearing thin clothes,      
He rowed the boat.      
But suddnely,      
His paddle broke into pieces.       
He turned to the exhausted and fallen Samayunkur,      
Seized the paddle of Samayunkur,      
Rowed the boat alone.      
      
Seeing this,       
My wicked thoughts stirred again.      
Yelling in a heavy tone, as if breaking wood into pieces.       
I ran up and down, back and forth, with light steps,       
Twisting my waist, swinging my limbs,      
Cheering on the Devil of Storm, devoting all my energy.      
      
Situation continued,       
The paddle of Samayunkur was also broken.      
Okikirmui turned to Shupunramka,       
Seized his paddle,      
And bravely rowed the boat.      
But the paddle of Shupunramka was also broken by the waves.      
      
Okikirmui stood in the boat.      
I never thought of,      
Okikirmui, as a human,      
Found me at the cape of the land, the cape of the gods,      
Amidst the fierce wind.      
He gazed into the center of my eyes.      
      
His face, once gentle, changed into anger.      
He searched inside his bag [[2]](#f_2_3).      
From his bag, he took out a small mugwort bow,      
And a small mugwort arrow.      
      
Seeing these, I laughed secretly.      
“Is there anything a human can do that is frightening?      
How can such a small mugwort arrow hurt? [[3]](#f_3_3)”      
      
Thinking this, at this cape,       
The cape of the land, the cape of the gods,      
I ran up and down, back and forth, with light steps,       
Twisting my waist, swinging my limbs,      
In a heavy tone, as if breaking wood into pieces,       
I cried, “Pau, pau!”      
Praising the Devil of Storm.      
Just then, Okikirmui fired the arrow.      
The arrow shot my neck hard.      
From then on, I knew nothing more.      
      
When I suddenly came to my senses,      
The weather was fine,      
The sea was calm.      
There was no sign of Okikirmui's boat.      
What had happened?       
From the top of my head,      
To the tip of my toes,      
I was completely in pain,       
As if the bark was burning and shrinking.      
I never imagined that,      
A small arrow from a human could cause such a great tortune to me.      
I strugged painfully.      
Upon this cape, the cape of the land, the cape of the gods,      
I cried and screamed,      
I strugged painfully.      
Days and nights, alive and dead.      
Finally I lost consciousness.      
      
I woke up again after an unknown period.      
I sat between the ears of a large black fox [[Note 2]](#f_additional_1_3)  .      
      
Two days later, Okikirmui came as if a god.      
He smilaed and said,      
“How beautiful! The black fox god who watches over      
The cape of the land, the cape of the gods,      
Had a good heart, a godly heart,      
So he died a beautiful death.”      
      
Saying this, he took my head to his house,       
Using the bone of my upper jaw as the foundation of his toilet,       
Using the bone of my lower jaw as the cornerstone of his wife's toilet [4].      
My remaining body rotted away with the earth.      
      
Days and nights, suffering from a foul odor,      
I died a meaningless death, a bad death.      
      
I was not a god with low status.      
But due to my terrible evil heart,      
I died a useless, bad death.      
      
From now on, foxes,       
Never hold evil in your hearts.      
      
Thia was a tale told by the Black Fox God about itself.

## Footnote        
      
### Chiri      
      
<span id="f_1_3">[1] pau: Onomatopoeia for the sound of a fox's cry. </span>      
      
<span id="f_2_3">[2] pushtotta: A bag-like item used when going out to sea for hunting, containing fire-making tools, medicines, and other necessary small items. Similar items for the same purpose include piuchiop and karop, but these are made from bulrush or attush weaving, so they are used on land. pushtotta is made from bear skin, seal skin, or other fur, making it waterproof and suitable for taking to sea. </span>      
      
<span id="f_3_3">[3] noya ai: Mugwort arrow. Mugwort is a revered plant among the Ainu. It is said that demons fear being struck by a mugwort arrow because they cannot float, making it an essential item when traveling far. </span>      
      
<span id="f_4_3">[4] Originally, men's and women's toilets were separate. ashinru and eosineru both refer to toilets.</span>      
      
<span id="f_additional_1_3">    Among foxes, the black fox is considered the most sacred. Capes protruding into the sea are generally the territory of black foxes, and it is said that a black fox will not let people hear its voice unless something very serious is happening.      
</span>        
      
<span id="f_additional_2_3">    Okikurumi (Okikirmui), Samayunkur, and Shupunramka are cousins, with Shupunramka being the oldest and Okikirmui the youngest. Shupunramka is a gentle and introverted person, so there is nothing to tell about him, but Samayunkur is short-tempered, lacks wisdom, is hasty, cowardly, and mean-spirited, while Okikirmui is wise like a god, compassionate, and courageous, so there are countless stories about him.      
</span>
